Generating sets explain how a group is built from a small stock of elements, and Cayley digraphs make that construction visible.

## Core definitions

- A subset $S$ of $G$ **generates** $G$ if every element of $G$ is a finite word in elements of $S$ and their inverses.
- $G$ is **finitely generated** if some finite set generates it.
- A **Cayley digraph** has one vertex per group element and edges recording multiplication by generators.

## High-yield facts

- $\langle S \rangle$ is the smallest subgroup containing $S$.
- Different generating sets can describe the same group.
- Adjacent transpositions generate $S_n$.

## Counterexamples worth remembering

- $(\mathbb{Q}, +)$ is not finitely generated.
- A generating set need not be minimal or unique.

## Toggleable proofs

> [!info]- Proof that < S > is the smallest subgroup containing S
>
> Let $\langle S \rangle$ denote the set of all finite words in elements of $S$ and their inverses. First we show it is a subgroup. It is nonempty because the empty word represents the identity. If
> $$
> x=s_1^{\varepsilon_1}\cdots s_r^{\varepsilon_r}
> \quad\text{and}\quad
> y=t_1^{\delta_1}\cdots t_m^{\delta_m},
> $$
> with each $s_i,t_j \in S$ and each exponent equal to $\pm 1$, then
> $$
> xy^{-1}=s_1^{\varepsilon_1}\cdots s_r^{\varepsilon_r}t_m^{-\delta_m}\cdots t_1^{-\delta_1}
> $$
> is again a finite word in elements of $S$ and their inverses. So $\langle S \rangle$ is a subgroup by the subgroup test.
>
> Now let $H$ be any subgroup of $G$ containing $S$. Since $H$ is closed under inverses and products, every finite word in elements of $S$ and their inverses lies in $H$. Therefore $\langle S \rangle \subseteq H$. So $\langle S \rangle$ is the smallest subgroup containing $S$.

> [!info]- Proof that (Q, +) is not finitely generated
>
> Assume for contradiction that $(\mathbb{Q}, +)$ is generated by finitely many rational numbers
> $$
> \frac{a_1}{b_1},\dots,\frac{a_r}{b_r},
> $$
> written in lowest terms with $b_i>0$. Let
> $$
> B=b_1b_2\cdots b_r.
> $$
> Any integer linear combination of the generators has denominator dividing $B$, because
> $$
> \sum_{i=1}^r n_i\frac{a_i}{b_i}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^r n_i a_i \frac{B}{b_i}.
> $$
> So every element of the subgroup generated by these rationals has the form $m/B$ for some integer $m$.
>
> Now choose a prime $p$ that does not divide $B$. Then $1/p \in \mathbb{Q}$, but $1/p$ cannot equal $m/B$ for any integer $m$, because that would imply $p \mid B$. This contradicts the assumption that the generators produce all of $\mathbb{Q}$. Therefore $(\mathbb{Q}, +)$ is not finitely generated.
