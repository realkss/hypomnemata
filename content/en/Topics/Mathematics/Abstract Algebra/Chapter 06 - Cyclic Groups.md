Cyclic groups are generated by a single element, and they are the cleanest groups for learning structure.

## Core definitions

- $G = \langle g \rangle$ means every element of $G$ is a power of $g$.
- The **order** of $g$ is the least positive $n$ with $g^n = e$, if such an $n$ exists.

## Key theorems

- Every cyclic group is abelian.
- Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic.
- In a cyclic group of order $n$, $\operatorname{ord}(g^k) = n / \gcd(n,k)$.
- A cyclic group of order $n$ has exactly $\varphi(n)$ generators.

## Counterexamples worth remembering

- $V_4 = \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ is abelian but not cyclic.
- Not every nonidentity element is a generator.
  In $\mathbb{Z}_6$, only $1$ and $5$ generate the whole group.

## Toggleable proofs

<details>
<summary>Proof that every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic</summary>

Let $G=\langle g \rangle$ and let $H \le G$. If $H=\{e\}$, then $H=\langle e \rangle$ is cyclic. Assume $H \ne \{e\}$. Consider the set
$$
S=\{m \in \mathbb{N} : g^m \in H\}.
$$
This set is nonempty because $H$ contains some nonidentity element $g^t$, and then either $t>0$ or $g^{-t} \in H$ with $-t>0$. By the well-ordering principle, $S$ has a least element $d$, so $g^d \in H$.

We claim that $H=\langle g^d \rangle$. Certainly $\langle g^d \rangle \subseteq H$ because $g^d \in H$ and $H$ is a subgroup. For the reverse inclusion, let $h \in H$. Since $G=\langle g \rangle$, we have $h=g^m$ for some integer $m$. Divide $m$ by $d$:
$$
m=qd+r,\qquad 0 \le r<d.
$$
Then
$$
g^r=g^{m-qd}=g^m(g^d)^{-q}.
$$
The right-hand side lies in $H$ because $g^m \in H$ and $g^d \in H$. By minimality of $d$, we must have $r=0$. Hence $m=qd$, so $h=g^m=(g^d)^q \in \langle g^d \rangle$. Therefore $H=\langle g^d \rangle$, and $H$ is cyclic.
</details>

<details>
<summary>Proof of the order formula $\operatorname{ord}(g^k)=n/\gcd(n,k)$</summary>

Assume $g$ has order $n$, and let $d=\gcd(n,k)$. Write $n=dn_1$ and $k=dk_1$ with $\gcd(n_1,k_1)=1$. Then
$$
(g^k)^{n/d}=g^{k(n/d)}=g^{dk_1n_1}=g^{k_1n}=e,
$$
so the order of $g^k$ divides $n/d$.

Now suppose $(g^k)^m=e$. Then $g^{km}=e$, so $n \mid km$. Since $n=dn_1$ and $k=dk_1$, this means
$$
dn_1 \mid dk_1m,
$$
hence $n_1 \mid k_1m$. Because $\gcd(n_1,k_1)=1$, Euclid's lemma gives $n_1 \mid m$. Therefore every exponent $m$ with $(g^k)^m=e$ is a multiple of $n_1=n/d$. So the least positive such $m$ is exactly $n/d$. Hence
$$
\operatorname{ord}(g^k)=\frac{n}{\gcd(n,k)}.
$$
</details>
